# Why Clinical EHR Patterns for Aged Care Complaints

## The Core Insight

Trilogy Care's workforce — Care Partners, Coordinators, clinical advisors — already thinks in clinical documentation patterns. They write SOAP notes, assess triage categories, follow care plans, and scan patient registers every day. A complaints system that mirrors these patterns requires **zero new mental models**.

## Rationale

### 1. Familiarity Reduces Training Time

Aged care staff rotate between clinical documentation (progress notes, care plans, incident reports) and administrative systems. When the complaints system uses the same structures — SOAP notes, triage categories, care plan checklists — staff can operate it competently from day one. There is no "learning the new system" because the patterns are already embedded in their professional practice.

### 2. Triage Categories Map Directly to ED Conventions

Australian aged care workers are trained in the Australasian Triage Scale during their clinical education. Mapping complaint urgency to a 5-category color-coded scale (Critical → Low) leverages this existing knowledge. A coordinator seeing a red Cat 1 dot knows instinctively that it demands immediate attention — no tooltip or legend needed.

### 3. Structured Documentation Reduces Errors

Free-text complaint notes are inconsistent and often incomplete. SOAP formatting forces comprehensive documentation:
- **Subjective** ensures the complainant's voice is captured
- **Objective** demands evidence and facts
- **Assessment** requires professional judgment (not just transcription)
- **Plan** mandates actionable next steps

This structure is proven across decades of clinical use to reduce documentation gaps and improve handover quality.

### 4. Data Density Serves Power Users

Clinical registers are intentionally dense. Staff who process dozens of complaints need to scan, filter, and prioritize quickly. Spacious card-based layouts that show 4-5 items per screen are inadequate for a 50+ record register. The clinical register pattern — compact rows, many columns, small text — lets experienced users see 15-20 records at once and make faster triage decisions.

### 5. Patient Banners Prevent Wrong-Record Errors

In clinical systems, the patient identification banner exists because wrong-patient errors can be fatal. In complaints management, wrong-record errors lead to misrouted responses, missed deadlines, and regulatory non-compliance. A persistent record banner (REF number, type, triage, assignee) ensures every action is taken on the correct complaint.

### 6. Care Plans Drive Accountability

Clinical care plans are the gold standard for tracking who needs to do what by when. Applying this pattern to complaint action items creates clear accountability: each action has an owner, a target date, and a status. Overdue items trigger the same clinical alert patterns (red highlighting, warning icons) that staff already respond to urgently in their clinical work.

## Trade-offs Acknowledged

- **Density may overwhelm new users**: The clinical register is optimised for experienced users. New staff may find it initially overwhelming compared to simpler card layouts.
- **SOAP structure adds friction**: Requiring four sections per note takes longer than free-text. The trade-off is completeness and consistency.
- **Clinical aesthetic may feel impersonal**: The no-nonsense clinical look prioritises function over warmth. For a complaints system that demands rigour and accountability, this is appropriate.

## Summary

Clinical patterns are not just a stylistic choice — they are a **cognitive ergonomics** decision. By matching the system's interaction patterns to the user's existing clinical training, we reduce errors, speed up processing, and ensure complaints are documented with the same rigour applied to clinical care.
