# Clinical Risk Domains — Whole-of-Client Perspective and Risk Mitigation

**Source**: Clinical Domains.docx (Maryanne Humphries, Feb 2026)
**Type**: Client-level clinical risk domain framework with mitigation effectiveness scoring
**Relationship to Risk Radar**: This IS the clinical model Risk Radar should implement. Replaces the simpler Prevalence × Impact model from the initial spec with a residual-risk model (consequence + mitigation = residual risk).

---

## Philosophy

Clinical risk domains assess the **whole client**, not isolated risks. While an individual domain may identify a high-level risk (e.g., a high fall risk), the broader domain assessment also considers protective factors and mitigating elements within the same functional or clinical context.

A client with an elevated falls risk may have intact mobility planning, good supervision, appropriate equipment, and no other functional impairments — resulting in a **lower overall clinical risk profile** than the falls score alone might suggest.

Care planning and escalation decisions are based on the **combined interaction of risks, strengths, and supports**, rather than a single domain driving an overly conservative or fragmented response.

---

## Cross-Domain Principles

- Clinical risks are assessed alongside mitigating factors for whole-of-client interpretation
- Isolated high risks are balanced against preserved function, stability, supports and active management
- Results in proportionate care planning and escalation aligned to the client's true overall clinical risk

---

## Mitigation Scoring (Universal)

Mitigation effectiveness is scored at 3 levels:

| Level | Score | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong mitigation** | 2 | Controls in place, used correctly, and adhered to |
| **Partial mitigation** | 1 | Controls present but inconsistently applied or emerging decline |
| **No mitigation** | 0 | Controls absent, refused, or ineffective |

Mitigation strategies are defined at domain level for consistency. Client-specific mitigation is documented within individual care plans.

## Domain 1: Functional Ability

**Why**: Functional decline is one of the strongest predictors of adverse outcomes in older adults, including hospitalisation, loss of independence and residential care entry. Functional impairment often precedes acute clinical deterioration.

**Risk areas**: Falls, Mobility, Continence, Cognition/Dementia

**Mitigation strategies**:
| Risk Area | Recognised Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Falls | Falls management plan; mobility aids correctly prescribed and used; home/environmental modifications; supervision for high-risk activities; client insight and adherence |
| Mobility | Appropriate equipment (walker, wheelchair); physiotherapy input; environmental access (ramps, rails); assistance available when required |
| Continence | Toileting routine; continence aids; skin integrity monitoring; timely assistance; infection prevention |
| Cognition/Dementia | Stable routines; prompts or cueing; supervision where needed; behavioural stability; informal or formal supports |
| Cross-cutting | No recent injurious falls; functional stability over time; effective monitoring and review |

**Mitigation decision prompts**:

- **Strong (2)**: Are appropriate supports in place and consistently used? Mobility aids correctly prescribed and used; environment assessed and hazards addressed; falls/continence plans in place and followed; client has insight and seeks assistance; no recent injurious falls or unmanaged functional incidents
- **Partial (1)**: Are supports present but not fully effective, inconsistently applied, or under review? Equipment exists but used inconsistently; plans documented but not always followed; early functional decline emerging; supervision variable
- **No mitigation (0)**: Are functional risks unmanaged or ineffective despite recommendations? No equipment or plans in place; client refuses or does not adhere; recurrent falls or functional incidents; decline is unmanaged or escalating

## Domain 2: Clinical Health Status

**Why**: Multimorbidity and medical instability are key drivers of morbidity, emergency presentations and mortality. Poorly controlled chronic disease, medication complexity and recurrent infections significantly increase adverse event likelihood.

**Risk areas**: Chronic Disease, Polypharmacy, Infection, Pain, End of Life

**Key principle**: Differentiates between **diagnosis and impact**. A client may have multiple conditions yet remain clinically stable, well managed, and functionally independent.

**Mitigation strategies**:
| Risk Area | Recognised Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Chronic Disease | Conditions clinically stable; GP-led management plan; regular monitoring; symptom control; adherence to treatment |
| Polypharmacy | Medication review completed; dose administration aid (DAA); supervision or delegation; clear medication responsibility |
| Infection | Infection prevention measures; early GP review; monitoring of symptoms; timely treatment; vaccination where relevant |
| Pain (Chronic) | Regular pain assessment; effective pain control plan; GP or specialist input; non-pharmacological strategies |
| End of Life | Advance care planning; palliative care involvement; symptom management plan; after-hours escalation pathway |

**Mitigation decision prompts**:

- **Strong (2)**: Chronic conditions controlled with clear GP-led plan; medications reviewed, supervised, or supported; infections promptly identified and treated; pain regularly assessed and controlled; advance care or palliative planning in place where relevant
- **Partial (1)**: Recent medication changes or emerging issues; intermittent infection or symptom flare-ups; pain or symptoms not yet optimally controlled; care plans exist but still bedding down
- **No mitigation (0)**: Poor disease control; recurrent infections or hospital presentations; medication mismanagement without oversight; refusal of treatment or absence of clinical planning

## Domain 3: Mental Health & Psychosocial

**Why**: Psychological and behavioural factors directly influence safety, adherence and care outcomes. Depression, anxiety, cognitive impairment and BPSD increase risk of falls, neglect, hospitalisation and care breakdown.

**Risk areas**: Mental Health, Behaviour, Insight, Supports

**Key principle**: Mental health and cognitive risks are **highly variable in functional impact**. A client may have a dementia diagnosis but demonstrate stable mood, effective communication, strong routines and supportive relationships.

**Mitigation strategies**:
| Risk Area | Recognised Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Mental Health | Mental health screening completed; stable mood; GP or mental health input; crisis plan if indicated |
| Behavioural Symptoms | Behavioural stability; triggers identified; support strategies in place; medication reviewed if relevant |
| Psychosocial Supports | Informal supports present; engagement with services; routines and meaningful activity |
| Insight & Engagement | Client understands risks; engages with care; communicates needs effectively |
| Risk Monitoring | No recent escalation, self-harm indicators, or behavioural incidents |

**Mitigation decision prompts**:

- **Strong (2)**: Mood and behaviour stable; supports and routines established and effective; client engages with care and understands risks; no recent distress, self-harm indicators, or behavioural escalation
- **Partial (1)**: Emerging anxiety, low mood, or behaviours; supports exist but effectiveness varies; engagement fluctuates; increased monitoring required
- **No mitigation (0)**: Acute distress or unmanaged behaviours; safety concerns for self or others; lack of insight or engagement; no support strategies in place

---

## Domain 4: Nutritional & Sensory Health

**Why**: Malnutrition, dehydration and sensory loss are independently associated with frailty, infection, falls, cognitive decline and mortality. Even modest unintentional weight loss or untreated oral disease can significantly worsen outcomes.

**Risk areas**: Nutrition, Hydration, Oral Health, Vision, Hearing

**Key principle**: Nutritional and sensory risks are often **modifiable and highly responsive to intervention**.

**Mitigation strategies**:
| Risk Area | Recognised Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Nutrition & Hydration | Stable weight; adequate intake; meal support; dietitian involvement where needed |
| Oral Health | Oral care routine; dental review if required; pain or infection addressed |
| Sensory Impairment | Glasses/hearing aids available and used; environment adapted; communication strategies |
| Monitoring | Regular weight or intake review; no signs of dehydration or malnutrition |

**Mitigation decision prompts**:

- **Strong (2)**: Weight and intake stable; meal supports or dietitian input in place if needed; oral health maintained; glasses/hearing aids available and used; no signs of dehydration or malnutrition
- **Partial (1)**: Intake fluctuates; sensory aids available but not consistently used; early weight loss or oral health concerns; monitoring underway
- **No mitigation (0)**: Weight loss, dehydration, or poor intake; untreated oral pain or infection; sensory impairment impacting safety; no monitoring or intervention

## Domain 5: Safety Risks

**Why**: Choking, pressure injuries and falls are among the leading causes of preventable harm and death in older populations. These are indicators of unmet care needs, reduced mobility, or gaps in supervision and clinical oversight.

**Risk areas**: Choking/Swallowing, Pressure Injuries, Environmental & Falls Safety

**Key principle**: Assesses **risk exposure alongside control measures**. A client may have an identified swallowing impairment or falls history, yet demonstrate effective mitigation through clinical assessment, modified diets, supervision, equipment and environmental controls.

**Mitigation strategies**:
| Risk Area | Recognised Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Choking/Swallowing | Speech pathology assessment completed; modified diet/fluids; supervision during meals; adherence to strategies |
| Pressure Injuries/Wounds | Skin integrity maintained; repositioning; wound care plan; equipment in place |
| Environmental Safety | Home safety assessment completed; hazards addressed; emergency planning |
| Falls Safety | Safety equipment; supervision during high-risk activities; emergency response plan |

**Mitigation decision prompts**:

- **Strong (2)**: Swallowing strategies assessed and followed; modified diets or supervision in place; skin integrity maintained with prevention strategies; environment assessed and hazards addressed; no recent safety incidents
- **Partial (1)**: Recommendations made but adherence variable; supervision inconsistent; early skin breakdown or safety concerns; monitoring increased
- **No mitigation (0)**: No swallowing assessment or diet modification despite risk; recurrent choking, wounds, or falls; unsafe environment; refusal or absence of controls

## Governance Safeguard (All Domains)

If your mitigation rating does not align with the domain risk score, **document the rationale** in the care plan or override field.

This protects:
- Clinical judgement
- Audit defensibility
- The residual-risk model

## Appendix — Worked Examples

### Example 1: Falls Risk — High Risk With Strong Mitigation

**Identified risk**: History of falls within last 6 months, moderate to high falls risk score.

**Mitigating factors**: Independently mobile with appropriate gait aids; falls management plan active and followed; home environment assessed and modified; intact cognition, good insight, seeks assistance; no other functional impairments.

**Interpretation**: Although falls sub-domain identifies elevated risk, strong functional capacity, environmental controls, supervision, and client insight significantly reduce likelihood and impact of harm. Overall clinical risk is **lower** than falls score alone would suggest.

### Example 2: Dementia — Moderate Cognitive Risk with Preserved Function

**Identified risk**: Early to moderate cognitive impairment, moderate dementia risk score.

**Mitigating factors**: Independent in ADLs; BPSD minimal and well managed; stable routines, familiar surroundings, consistent informal supports; effective communication.

**Interpretation**: Dementia present but functional impact currently limited. Preserved function, stable behaviour, and strong psychosocial supports mitigate overall clinical risk. Supports ongoing monitoring rather than higher-level intervention.

### Example 3: Choking — Identified Risk With Effective Controls

**Identified risk**: History of swallowing difficulty, moderate choking risk score.

**Mitigating factors**: Speech pathology assessment completed; modified diet and fluid consistency in place and adhered to; eats slowly, supervised during meals, understands safe swallowing strategies; no recent choking incidents; nutrition and hydration stable.

**Interpretation**: Swallowing impairment exists but actively managed. Likelihood of serious harm reduced. Overall clinical risk remains proportionate and controlled.
