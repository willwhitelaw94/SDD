# RIN Panel — Loom Walkthrough Script (V3)

> **Duration target**: 9–10 minutes
> **Presenter**: Ed
> **Screen**: `sidebar-playground.html` open in browser, v3 selected, Scenario: Happy path, both panels closed initially
> **Context**: V2 introduced the conversation guide structure and planted the seed that v3 would reframe around client experience. This walkthrough is honest about where the lens has actually shifted and where it hasn't yet. The biggest structural change: the panel has split into two — Relationship Intelligence (reference card) and Conversation Guide (call companion). Also introduces scenario flexibility, touchpoint enhancements aligned to Support at Home care management billing, and auth rep handling.
> **Caveat**: Still exploration — not a spec, not a commitment.

---

**[Screen: Overview tab, both panels closed — normal portal view]**

> "Last time I walked through v2 of the Relationship Intelligence panel. We introduced the three-phase conversation guide — opening, steering to business, handling conflict — and got good feedback on the structure."
>
> "I also planted a seed: that v3 would start to reframe this initiative around client experience rather than coordinator tooling. I want to be honest about where that lens has actually shifted and where it hasn't yet."
>
> "Quick context before we dive in: Will's team is building a dedicated workflow for scheduled client check-ins — that's a structured process for managing your contact cadence. What I'm showing you today isn't trying to replace that. This lives on the package page — where you already are when you're pulling up a client's details and prepping for a call. It's an ad hoc companion for when you're about to pick up the phone, not a workflow engine."

## 2. The North Star — And Where We Actually Are (40 sec)

**[Screen: Still on Overview tab — no panels yet, just talking]**

> "The north star for v3 is: *does Margaret feel known every time we call?* The coordinator is the vehicle, the client experience is the destination."
>
> "But here's the honest version: most of what you're about to see is still coordinator tooling. Better structured, more useful, aligned to Support at Home — but still tools for the person making the call."
>
> "There are two features in v3 that I think genuinely shift toward client experience. I'll call them out when we get there. Everything else is solid operational improvement — and that matters — but I don't want to dress it up as something it isn't yet."

## 3. The Big Structural Change — Two Panels (40 sec)

**[Click the Intelligence icon on the tool strip — panel opens]**

> "The first thing you'll notice: the single panel has split into two. This is the Relationship Intelligence panel — your reference card. Essentials, alerts, personal context, and touchpoint logging. Everything you need to know *about* the client."

**[Click the Conversation Guide icon — second panel opens alongside]**

> "And this is the Conversation Guide — your call companion. Opening phrases, personal touch prompts, transition phrases, your checklist, and the conflict framework. Everything you need *during* the call."
>
> "The split is a UX decision, not a client experience one. Margaret doesn't know or care how many panels we have. But it makes the coordinator more effective — reference card before and after the call, guide open during."

---

## 4. Last Conversation — Know Before You Dial (30 sec)

**[Focus on the Conversation Guide panel — Last Conversation card at the top]**

> "Before we get to the call itself — the guide now opens with context from the last conversation. Who spoke to Maggie, when, how long, what the sentiment was, and the key notes."
>
> "So here: Levi spoke to her on the 18th of Feb, 22-minute call, went well. Monthly check-in, Monitoring and Review. Grandkids visiting more often, tomatoes doing beautifully."
>
> "This is the context you need *before* you open your mouth. You know what was discussed, you know the tone, and you know what personal threads to pick up on. Without this, you're going in blind — or you're spending two minutes scrolling through notes trying to piece it together."

## 6. Personal Touch — This Is the Lens Shift (50 sec)

**[Scroll to or point at the Personal Touch section]**

> "OK — this is the first feature I think genuinely shifts the lens toward client experience. Personal Touch. These are prompts pulled from what we already know about Margaret — her last conversation, her upcoming events — surfaced at exactly the moment they're useful."
>
> "Levi mentioned last call that Sarah's been bringing the grandkids over every Sunday. The system surfaces: 'Last time we chatted you mentioned Sarah's been bringing the kids over every Sunday — how's that going?' The tomatoes came up too: 'How are the tomatoes going? I heard you've been giving them to the neighbours!' Her birthday is in 6 days: 'Happy birthday for Saturday, Maggie! Any plans?'"
>
> "Without this, these threads get lost. Not because coordinators don't care — because they're making 30 calls a day across a large caseload. The grandkids detail is buried in last call's notes. The birthday is in a field no one checks. And Margaret notices. She notices when no one remembers what she told them last time. She notices when her birthday passes without a mention."
>
> "That's why these are labelled 'Personal touch' — they're optional rapport builders, not required actions. But they're the features most directly tied to whether Margaret feels known."

**[Tick one of the cards to show the checked state]**

> "Tick them as covered — they fade but stay visible. Dismiss with the X if not relevant."

**[Click '+ Add personal touch', type 'Mentioned new puppy called Biscuit', click Add]**

> "And here's the second part I think matters: you can capture context mid-call. 'Mentioned new puppy called Biscuit.' This saves to the guide *and* adds it as an interest on the Intelligence panel."

**[Point at the new pill that appeared in Interests on the Intelligence panel]**

> "So next time *anyone* calls Maggie — not just Levi, anyone — they'll know about Biscuit. That's relationship continuity across the team, not just within one coordinator's memory. That's a client experience outcome."

## 9. The Guide Flexes — Scenario Switcher (60 sec)

**[Point at the Scenario dropdown in the controls bar, currently on "Happy path"]**

> "So everything you've just seen is the happy path — Maggie's doing well, good last call, nice personal threads to pick up on. But not every call looks like that. So I want to show you how the guide adapts."

**[Switch to "Frustrated client"]**

> "This is a very different call. Last conversation was 38 minutes with Amy — sentiment: Concerned. Maggie's had her third care partner change in four months, she's raised a formal complaint, and her daughter Sarah has called separately to express concern. And *you're* the new care partner — you've never spoken to Maggie before."

> "Look at how the guide has shifted. The opening carousel doesn't say 'Morning Maggie, it's Levi' — it says 'Hi Maggie, I'm Priya, I'm your new care partner.' It acknowledges the disruption upfront. The personal touch cards aren't about tomatoes — they're about the complaint, about Sarah's concern, and one thread from her interests to bring some warmth into a difficult conversation."

> "The checklist is completely different too — complaint progress, confirming the schedule, care partner preferences. And notice: the conflict framework is *open by default*. Because in this call, you're probably going to need it."

**[Switch to "New client"]**

> "And here's a third scenario — brand new client. First follow-up after onboarding. Last call was 45 minutes, sentiment neutral — she seemed overwhelmed. The openers are gentle: 'I know we covered a lot last time, just wanted to check in.' Personal touch asks how she's settling in, mentions cooking and her cat Whiskers. Checklist is all onboarding items — week 1 review, missing contacts, booking the first formal review."

**[Switch back to "Happy path"]**

> "Same structure, same sections, completely different content. The guide adapts to the situation — it's not a one-size-fits-all script."

## 11. Touchpoint Logging — Support at Home Aligned (40 sec)

**[Switch back to Intelligence panel, expand the Touchpoint section, click 'Log Touchpoint']**

> "Enhanced touchpoint logging — this is operational and commercial, not client experience. But it matters for the business case."
>
> "You now capture call duration. 15 minutes or more flags as a potential care management activity under Support at Home. You select a Primary Activity from five categories aligned to the billing framework: Monitoring and Review, Care Planning, Organising and Adjusting Services, Navigating Options, and Supporting Feedback and Complaints."

**[Select an activity, point at the duration badge]**

> "This is informational — it doesn't trigger billing. But it makes visible that the relationship-building work coordinators do has revenue value. That's important for the business case: every meaningful touchpoint has revenue potential, so making touchpoints easier and more frequent has concrete ROI."

---

## 12. Sentiment — The Second Lens Shift (30 sec)

**[Point at the sentiment row at the bottom of the touchpoint form]**

> "And this is the second feature I think genuinely shifts the lens. A one-tap sentiment indicator after the call: Happy, Neutral, or Concerned. Completely optional."
>
> "Right now, our compliance dashboards measure contact *frequency* — did we call? How often? Sentiment measures contact *quality* — how did it go? Three 'Concerned' ratings in a row is a signal that something's wrong in the relationship, not just the operations."
>
> "I'll be honest — this is a coordinator self-report, not a client measure. It's a proxy. But it's the first time we'd be capturing *any* signal about relationship quality. And it opens the door to real client experience metrics down the track — things like post-call satisfaction, relationship continuity scoring, follow-through tracking."

## 14. Where the Lens Hasn't Shifted Yet (40 sec)

**[Both panels visible]**

> "So I said I'd be honest about where the lens has and hasn't shifted. What's genuinely client-experience-oriented: Personal Touch prompts, mid-call context capture that syncs to the profile, and sentiment tracking. Those three features are designed around whether Margaret feels known."
>
> "What's still coordinator and operational tooling: the panel split, the checklist, transition phrases, duration tracking, activity categories. All useful — but Margaret doesn't experience any of that directly."
>
> "Where I think the lens shifts further in future iterations: follow-through accountability — did we actually ask about the grandkids, or just see the prompt and skip it? Relationship continuity — how many different coordinators has Maggie spoken to this month, and did they all have context? And eventually, client-visible measurement — not just our self-report, but actual client feedback."
>
> "v3 plants the seeds. The operational tooling is strong. The client experience features are real but early. The next iteration is where we start measuring outcomes from Margaret's perspective, not just ours."

---

## 15. Wrapping Up (20 sec)

> "So to recap: two-panel split, Personal Touch prompts, scenario-aware conversation guides, call checklist, auth rep handling, Support at Home-aligned touchpoint logging, and sentiment tracking. The tools serve the coordinator. Two features genuinely serve the client. The guide flexes to the situation. And the roadmap is clear for pushing that further."
>
> "Keen for feedback — especially on whether Personal Touch and sentiment feel like the right starting points for the client lens, whether the frustrated-client scenario feels realistic, and what's missing. Let me know."

## Notes for Recording

- **Start with both panels closed** — show the "before" state
- **Open Intelligence first, then Guide** — demonstrate the two-panel concept clearly
- **Be explicit about what is and isn't a lens shift** — this is the differentiator from v2's script. Don't claim everything is client-experience. Call out the two features that genuinely are (Personal Touch, Sentiment) and be honest that the rest is operational
- **"Margaret notices"** — the grandkids/tomatoes thread is the emotional core. These are things she *told* us — and she'll notice if no one remembers. Pause on it. Let it land.
- **Personal touch capture syncing to interests** — this is the "aha" moment. Point at the pill appearing on the Intelligence panel. Say "relationship continuity across the team"
- **Sentiment framing** — be honest it's a proxy, a self-report. But frame it as "the first time we'd capture any quality signal"
- **Scenario switcher is the crowd-pleaser** — switch between happy/frustrated/new-client live. The frustrated scenario is the one that lands hardest — "you're brand new, she's furious, and the guide has your back." Don't rush it.
- **Auth rep is quick** — just show the name swap and the banner. Don't linger.
- **Section 14 is new and important** — the "where the lens hasn't shifted yet" section builds credibility. It shows you're thinking critically, not just marketing features
- **Future vision items** — follow-through accountability, relationship continuity scoring, client-visible measurement. Plant these seeds but don't promise them
- **Touchpoint/billing section** — frame as "business case", not "client experience". Don't conflate revenue awareness with client outcomes
- **Energy** — confident but self-aware. "Here's what we've done, here's what it actually means, here's where it's going." Not overselling.
- **Don't demo Call Bridge** — it's still there but off-topic for this version
- **Caveat** — "exploring", "iterating", "early days". Not "building" or "shipping"
- **No names** — don't name the research participant or specific coordinators
- **Keep it under 10 minutes** — scenario demo and auth rep add ~90 seconds but they're worth it. The honesty section (14) earns trust.
